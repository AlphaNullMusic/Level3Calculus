Complex Numbers

- Loci

As complex numbers are able to be represented on argand diagrams, different geometrical shapes and regions can be formed by complex equations (or inequations).

Complex equations can limit movement along a set of points that meet a specific requirement (a locus). Some equations create loci that can form straight lines, circles or ellipses. You need to be able to identify different complex equations that will give different basic shapes.


The equation \(|z-z_{0}| = r\) gives a circle locus, where \(r\) is the radius of the circle, \(z\) is a point on the circle and \(z_{0}\) is the coordinate of the centre of the circle.

This can be proven if we let \(z=x+yi\). This rearranged into the following equations: \((x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2\), which is the general formula for a circle with (a,b) as the centre coordinate and a radius of \(r\).

Example of circular locus:

The complex equation \(|z+3+2i|=3\) will produce a circle of the form: \(|z-z_{0}=r|\). Therefore: \(|z-(-3-2i)|=3\).

The centre of the circle is at \((-3-2i)\) and the radius is 3 units.

There are three general equations for when a locus of points forms a straight line:

  1. a vertical line where \(x=a\), where \(a\) is the x-axis intercept.
  2. a horizontal line where \(y=b\), where \(b\) is the y-axis intercept.
  3. the general equation of a line which is \(y=mx+c\) or \(ax+by+c=0\).

Example of straight-line locus:

• Give the locus of points for the equation: \(|z-2+i| = |z+1|\).

First we replace \(z\) with \((x+yi)\): \[x + yi - 2 + i = x + yi + 1\] \[x - 2 + yi + i = x + 1 + yi\] \[x - 2 + i(y + 1) = x + 1 + yi\]

Then we square both sides and simplify: \[\sqrt{(x-2)^2 + (y+1)^2} = \sqrt{(x+1)^2 + (y)^2}\] \[(x-2)^2 + (y+1)^2 = (x+1)^2 + (y)^2\] \[x^2 - 4x + 4 + y^2 + 2y + 1 = x^2 + 2x + 1 + y^2\] \[6x - 2y - 4 = 0\]

Finally, we can rearrange it in \(y=mx+c\) form: \[y = 3x - 2\]

Therefore, the locus will have a gradient of 3 and a y-intercept of -2, which can be represented on an argand diagram.
