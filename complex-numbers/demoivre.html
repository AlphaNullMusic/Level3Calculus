Complex
Numbers

- De Moivre's Theorem

Let's try squaring the complex number \(z=r\text{cis}(\theta)\): \[\eqalign{z^2 &= (r\text{cis}(\theta))^2 \\ &= (r\text{cis}(\theta))(r\text{cis}(\theta)) \\ &= r^2 \text{cis}(2\theta)}\]

De Moivre's Theorem generalises this result to any power (\(n\)). It states that if \(z=r\text{cis}(A)\), then \(z^n = r^n \text{cis}(nA)\). We can use this to find power and roots of complex numbers in polar form.


Example:

• Find \(z^4\) when \(z=5\text{cis}(1.5)\). Express your answer in rectangular form.

\[\eqalign{z^4 &= (5\text{cis}(1.5))^4 \\ &= 5^4(\text{cis}(1.5*4)) \\ &= 625\text{cis}(6)}\]

Because the angle (6) is greater than \(\pi\) we have to simplify. We can do this by subtracting the angle from \(2\pi\) (a full revolution). This means that the answer is: \(z^4 = 625\text{cis}(-0.2832)\).


The final answer in rectangular form is: \(z^4 = 600.1 - 174.6i\)
