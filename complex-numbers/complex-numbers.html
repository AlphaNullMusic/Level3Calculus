Complex Numbers

- Complex Numbers

There are some problems that have no real solutions. For example, to solve \(x^2=-1\) for x, we take the square root of both sides, which leaves \(x=\sqrt{-1}\).

The problem here is that there is no number that multiplies by itself to equal -1. To find an answer for this problem, we have to extend the domain of our number system out of just Real Numbers.

We can designate \(\sqrt{-1}\) as \(i\), which is the imaginary number. We can also multiply \(i\) by itself to get -1, a real number:

\[\eqalign{i^2 &= i*i \\ &=\sqrt{-1}*\sqrt{-1} \\ &=-1}\]
This allows us to find the square root of any negative number.

A number that is part real and part imaginary is called a Complex Number. It is often denoted as \(z\), instead other variables.

For example: \(z=3+2i\)


There are two rules that apply to arithmetic with complex numbers.


  1. Add or subtract the real numbers together, and add or subtract the imaginary numbers together.

    Example: \[\eqalign{(7+2i) + (1+3i) &= (7+1) + (2i+3i) \\ &= 8+5i}\]

  2. If multiplying two imaginary numbers together, the result will always be real as \(i^2=-1\).

    Example: \[\eqalign{3i*8i &= 24i^2 \\ &= -24}\]

- Complex Conjugates

The complex conjugate of a complex number has the same imaginary and real parts, but an opposite sign. If \(z=a+bi\), then its complex conjugate is \(\bar{z}=a-bi\), where \(\bar{z}\) denotes the conjugate of \(z\).


Example question:

If \(x=2+3i\) and \(y=2-3i\), then calculate \(xy\).

First, we see that y is the conjugate of x, since they have the same imaginary and real parts but with a different sign. Therefore, we can rewrite the question as: \(xy=(2+3i)(2-3i)\).

We can now expand out the brackets to solve the equation: \[\eqalign{(2+3i)(2−3i) &= (2*2)+(2*−3i)+(2*3i)+(3i*−3i) \\ &= 4-9i^2 \\ &= 4+9 \\ &= 13}\]

When a complex number and its conjugate are added to each other, the result is always a real number.
