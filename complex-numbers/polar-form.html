Complex Numbers

- Argand Diagrams

We can't represent complex numbers on a normal number line. This is because they are made up of real and imaginary numbers, so we have to use a two-dimensional representation.

To do this, we can graph a cartesian plane with the imaginary component on the vertical axis and the real component on the horizontal axis, sometimes referred to as a complex plane.

Then, we can draw coordinates from the origin (0,0) to the coordinate of the complex number. The graph is called an Argand Diagram. You may be expected to draw one in the exam.

Example:

• Represent the complex number \(2+3i\) on an argand diagram.

To do this, we would go three units up in imaginary axis and along 2 units in the real axis. We can then draw a straight line to this point.

- Modulus

In the argand diagram above, the length from the origin to the complex number is labelled \(r\). This is known as the modulus, or absolute value. As suggested in the name, it's the absolute value from (0,0) to the complex number on the two-dimensional plane. The absolute value of a complex number is often shown as \(|a+bi|\).


Example:

• Calculate \(|2+i|\).

To solve this, we have to use Pythagoras' Theorem on the right-angled triangle that we can see on the argand diagram: \[\eqalign{r^2 &= a^2 + b^2 \\ &= 2^2 + 1^2 \\ &= 5}\] \[r=\sqrt{5}\]

- Argument

The angle between the modulus and the positive real axis is referred to as the argument (symbol \(\theta\)). We can use trigonometry on the right-angled triangle formed on the argand diagram to find the angle.


Example:

• Calculate the argument of \(2+i\): \[\tan(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\] \[\eqalign{\theta &= \arctan{\frac{1}{2}} \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \text{radians}}\]

- Converting to Polar Form

Until now, we have been using rectangular form for these calculations. However, we can convert complex numbers to polar form, and vice versa. To do this, we first need to calculate both the modulus and the argument.

We can derive the formula for complex numbers in polar form using trigonometric ratios.


For \(a\) (the real part of the number): \[\cos(\theta)=\frac{a}{r}\] \[a=r\cos(\theta)\]


For \(bi\) (the imaginary part of the number): \[\sin(\theta)=\frac{b}{r}\] \[b=r\sin(\theta)\] \[bi=i*r\sin(\theta)\]


We can then substitute these into the general expression for complex numbers: \[\eqalign{z &= a+bi \\ &= r\cos(\theta) + ir\sin(\theta) \\ &= r(\cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta)) \\ &= r\text{cis}(\theta) }\] Where \(\text{cis}(\theta) = \cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta)\)


Example of conversion to polar form:

• Convert \(z=5-2i\) to polar form.

First, we calculate the modulus: \[\eqalign{r &= \sqrt{5^2 + (-2)^2} \\ &= \sqrt{25 + 4} \\ &= \sqrt{29}}\]

Then we calculate the argument: \[\eqalign{\theta &= \arctan(\frac{2}{5}) \\ &= 21.8 \text{ degrees (or 0.3805 rad)}}\] Because the complex number is below the real axis (negative imaginary part), the argument is expressed as negative. Therefore, we can rewrite in polar form:

  • - Rectangular form: \(z=5-2i\)
  • - Polar Form: \(z=\sqrt{29}\text{cis}(-0.3805)\text{ rad}\)

Polar form is either expressed in radians or degrees (depending on how you calculated the modulus and argument). If in radians, it is commonly expressed to four decimal places, and one for degrees.


Example of conversion to rectangular form:

We can also convert complex numbers in polar form back to rectangular form. This is done by splitting \(\text{cis}(\theta)\) into \(\cos(\theta)+i\sin(\theta)\) to get the real and imaginary components.


• Express \(z=2\text{cis}(100)\) in rectangular form.

\[\eqalign{z &= 2\text{cis}(100) \\ &= 2\cos(100) + 2i\sin(100) \\ &= -0.35 + 1.97i}\]

- Multiplying and Dividing

To multiply complex numbers in polar form, we multiply the moduli and add the arguments. So, the formula is \(zw=r_{1}r_{2}\text{cis}(A+B)\), where \(z\) and \(w\) are complex numbers in polar form.


To divide complex numbers in polar form, we divide the moduli and subtract the arguments. This makes the formula to divide: \(\frac{z}{w} = \frac{r_{1}}{r_{2}}\text{cis}(A-B)\).

Example:

• If \(z=6\text{cis}(2.25)\) and \(p=2\text{cis}(1.55)\), calculate \(\frac{z}{p^2}\) to two decimal places.

\[\eqalign{\frac{z}{p^2} &= \frac{6\text{cis}(2.25)}{(2\text{cis}(1.55))(2\text{cis}(1.55))} \\ &= \frac{6\text{cis}(2.25)}{4\text{cis}(3.1)} \\ &= (6 \div 4)\text{cis}(2.25 - 3.1) \\ &= 1.5\text{cis}(-0.85) \text{ rad}}\]
